2. Problem Statement¶

The technical analysis of historical stock market data is used to study future market behavior. Analyzing stocks helps investors find the best investment opportunities. The two most common forms of technical analysis are through plots and statistical indicators.

In Europe, Joseph de la Vega adopted early technical analysis techniques to predict Dutch markets in the 17th century. In its modern form, however, technical analysis owes heavily to Charles Dow [1], William P. Hamilton, Robert Rhea, Edson Gould, and many others [2] —including a ballroom dancer named Nicolas Darvas. These people represented a new perspective on the market as a tide that is best measured in highs and lows on a chart rather than by the particulars of the underlying company.

Confidence in the stability of future investments is reliant on data. Investors usually use a mathematical approach to take in past behaviors to forecast future outcomes. Exploratory data analysis is the initial step of developing mathematical models. Exploratory data analysis is a task performed by data scientists to get familiar with the data.

[1] https://www.investopedia.com/articles/financial-theory/08/charles-dow.asp

[2] https://www.investopedia.com/articles/financial-theory/10/pioneers-technical-analysis.asp

Data description:

The stock market data is in the .csv file, the name of this file is the financials.csv. Please download both files from the canvas. Most variables/features name's in the stock market data file are self-explanatory.

Columns in data:

  • Company_Name: Name of the company

  • Stock_Market_Sector: A stock market sector is a group of stocks with a lot in common, usually, because they are in similar industries and sometimes identical business models.

  • Price: Price is the most recent stock selling price.

  • Price/Earnings: Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio shows what the market is willing to pay for a stock based on its past or future earnings. A high P/E could mean that a stock's price is higher than earnings and possibly overvalued. Conversely, a low P/E might indicate that the current stock price is low relative to earnings.

  • Dividend Yield: Dividend yield is a stock's annual dividend payments to shareholders expressed as a percentage of the stock's current price. It is the percentage of a company's share price that it pays out in dividends each year.

  • Earnings/Share: Earnings per share (EPS) is a company's net profit divided by the number of common shares it has outstanding. It is how much money a company makes for each share of its stock and is a widely used metric for estimating corporate value.

  • 52 Week Low: The data point includes the lowest price at which a stock has traded during the previous 52 weeks. Many investors prefer to buy undervalued stocks, as it is believed that there is a high chance of such stocks to go higher in the future.

  • 52 Week High: The data point includes the highest price at which a stock has traded during the previous 52 weeks.

  • Market_Cap: Market cap—or market capitalisation—refers to the total value of all a company's shares of stock.

  • EBITDA: EBITDA, or earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortisation, is a measure of a company's overall financial performance and is used as an alternative to the net income in some circumstances. EBITDA, however, can be misleading because it strips out the cost of capital investments like property, plant, and equipment. https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/ebitda.asp

  • Price/Sales: The price-to-sales (P/S) ratio shows how much investors are willing to pay per dollar of sales for a stock. A low ratio could imply the stock is undervalued, while a higher-than-average ratio could indicate that the stock is overvalued. https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/price-to-salesratio.asp

  • Price/Book: The P/B ratio measures the market's valuation of a company relative to its book value. A company's book value is the difference between its total assets and total liabilities and not its share price in the market. https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/price-to-bookratio.asp
